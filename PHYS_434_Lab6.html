It can be clearly observed that our pdf drops below zero. This means that if an observed signal has a strength of 110, the probability distribution for our true signal would have positive values below 0. However, we know that our actual signal is positive. This will cause difficulties in calculating probabilities and statistical thresholds. One way that we can calculate statistical thresholds is not by normalizing our pdf, but by normalizing the value that we put into our calculations. Below we initially calculate our 0.95 upper probability threshold, and find it to be 25.6001. After normalizing our distribution data and summing over values below 25.6001, we only found a cumulative probability of 0.8181. We then calculated integral of the pdf that was greater than 0. To normalize the probability of 0.95, we multiplied 0.95 by 1 and divided it by the value of the integral. From this we calculated a value for a 0.95 cumulative probability of 33.2332. After summing our normalized distribution data over values below 33.2332, we found a cumulative probability of 0.9667. This is reasonably close enough to our desired value of 0.95 to confirm that this is a valid method of normalizing our statistical threshold to account for the part of our pdf below 0.
Because of this, this distribution shows both a severe lack of symmetry and a large bias. The lower tail of the distribution below 0 being cut off categorizes it as nonsymmetric. This also creates a bias in the distribution towards greater values than our expected mean of 110-mean=10.02. This has been shown in our need to weight our probability to properly account for this bias when calculating the 0.95 probability threshold above.
